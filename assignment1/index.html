1.Flatness

From inside the cave...

The world is simple. We, the dwellers, spend our days watching the shadows dance across the stone wall before us, interpreting their movements as signs, omens, or even living beings. One shadow suddenly twists and rotates along its axis, its edges distorting unpredictably. Some of us whisper that it must be a divine figure, a spirit twisting in response to unseen forces. Others insist it is merely a trick of fate, a restless being caught in an eternal cycle of motion. Then, another shadow behaves strangely. Instead of merely shifting in place, it slides back and forth, almost as if trying to escape. The cave dwellers panic. "It is breaking free!" one shouts. "The world beyond must be crumbling!" another cries. But we fail to realize that these movements are not the actions of the shadows themselves but rather the result of something outside our view—something controlling them in ways we cannot comprehend. Our understanding of reality is limited, shaped entirely by the shifting patterns of light and darkness that we mistake for truth.

This is what Nick Sousanis calls Flatness—a one-dimensional way of thinking where knowledge is confined to a singular plane of experience. Flatness represents the unwillingness, or inability, to perceive a world beyond the immediate and the familiar. In the cave, we interpret the changing shadows as independent events, unaware they merely reflect a more significant reality hidden beyond our sight. Our truth is narrow, yet, to us, it is absolute.

2.Seeinng Double

From outside the cave...

The air is crisp and filled with light. No longer bound by darkness, I can finally see the whole picture—the fundamental forms of the objects that had once cast the flickering shadows within the cave. Before me stand two solid structures, their edges sharp and defined, no longer blurred silhouettes. The truth is unmistakable: the shadows on the wall were only projections of these objects, distorted by distance and perspective. As the light shifts, one of the objects begins to rise, moving upward in smooth, deliberate motion. At the same time, the second object moves with it, creating a perfect harmony between their positions. The rising shadows would have been interpreted inside the cave as a celestial event, perhaps even a divine ascension. But here, outside, I see the reality: the objects are simply responding to the natural rhythm of motion and light. Then, something even more striking happens. The first structure, which had remained rigid, begins to rotate—its entire form twisting in all directions as if awakening to its movement. The second and third structures follow, their rotations synchronized, revealing an intricate dance of motion and balance. Had the cave dwellers seen this, they would have been terrified, believing the very foundation of their world was shifting. But here, outside, it is evident: movement is natural. Change is inevitable.

Seeing Double is the ability to perceive multiple layers of reality at once—to recognize that what we see is often a fragmented version of a more profound truth. We saw only the shadows inside the cave, disconnected from their origins. Outside, I see both the shadows and their sources, understanding how they are connected. The contrast between the two worlds is striking: what was once a flickering illusion is now a tangible, observable reality. Ultimately, the journey from the cave to the outside world is not just physical but a thought transformation. To remain in the cave is to accept Flatness, to mistake shadows for truth. To leave the cave is to embrace Seeing Double, to understand that knowledge is layered and that there is always more beyond what is immediately visible.
